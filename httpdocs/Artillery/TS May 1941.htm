War Diary

 

384th Field Battery RA 142nd Field Regiment RA

 

PLACE

DATE

 

 

Basildon

 

… Sgt Harman of 384 Battery with Lt. Donald 506 Third. Sunny cold day.

 

CC

 

4 May 1941

Morning spent in loading for the “REPUSE” exercise. A fine day.

 

 

 

5

Loading was continued in the morning and the Battery was ordered to a hide North of Henley under command of the 6th Devons. At 1515 the forward body moved off, “C” Troop was detailed, and at 1545 the main body left. The Battalion then moved to the concentration area just north of Overton, where they remained for about two hours. At about 1930 hrs we moved again to an unknown destination. Eventually on the line of march a message was received from the Battalion Commander that we were to take up a position in the neighbourhood of Redlynch. The message was rather vague and was not received by the Battery Commander until he was within a few miles of the destination. When we arrived at Redlynch it was dark and the Battalion Commander said that it was not necessary to get the guns into action that night.

The Battery harboured in Redlynch that night, the majority of the Battery sleeping in The village hall.

 

CC

Redlynch

6

A conference was held at RHQ at 0030 hrs at which 384 Bty was placed in support of the 9th Devons. We were to supply continuous support while the 9th advanced next day. Things did not start very smoothly the next morning as the Devon’s only had about a mile between their forward body and main body all of which were advancing along the same road. The Battery was expected to leapfrog up behind the forward body and of course this did not allow the Battery enough freedom of movement and the rear troop was held up by the main body. However after about an hour things settled down and the rear troop was able to leapfrog up and the Battery was able to give continuous support until the evening. At about 1430 hrs the 9th Devons gained their final line without very much opposition. At dusk we were ordered to reconnoitre a rear position as the Infantry felt they could not hold the present line. However the rear position was never occupied and 0200 hrs we were ordered to withdraw to a Regimental hide N.E. of Redlynch. Battery Headquarters at 0145, C. Tp at 0200 hrs and D Troop at 0230 hrs. A fine sunny day.

 

CC

 

7

At about 0600 hrs the Battery took up a position overlooking the river Test and remained there under regimental control until the following morning. During the day one or two concentrations were fired, but very little enemy activity was observed from the OPs and there was no observed shooting. A fine day. At 1130 orders were received at RHQ that a predicted smoke screen was to be fired at 0515 hrs the next morning. The trace from Div. HQ was handed over.

 

CC

 

8

The predicted smoke screen was worked out by the CPO and surveyors. It was felt that it was incomplete as there were no grid intersections marked on it to enable it to be positioned and the Screen was in fact begun before it was light and would have lost a good deal of its effectiveness, and probably would have been a waste of smoke. Today the Battery provided a forward body troop to the 9th Devons who advanced south through Fordingbridge. Continuous support was not quite as effective as it might have been as we were still under Regimental control and orders did not get through as quickly as they would had we been under Command of the 9th Devons. The cease fire went at about 1.30 hrs and the Regiment moved to a concentration area East of Fordingbridge. We moved back to Basildon in Bde Column at 1700hrs. A fine day.

 

CC

Basildon

9

The day was spent in maintenance as all equipment had been through a good deal. However vehicles were found to have suffered very little.

 

CC

 

10

The regiment moved at 0900 hrs on its way to Norfolk for three weeks to relieve three Regiments who were going to practice camp. The first night was spent in Bivouac N.E. of Newmarket. The route up was through Henley, Marlow,Berkhampstead, Luton, Royston, Newmarket. A fine day. Sharp frost at night.

 

CC

Hargham

11

Moved into Hargham camp arriving at 1300 hrs. The tentage at the camp is good and there is a large house which accommodates the Officers mess and RHQ offices. We took over from the 71st Fd. Regt. A fine day.

 

CC

 

12

Contacted the 2/5 Leicesters at Norwich and discussed the operational role of the Battery, also visited the lying up area at Weston Hall, which provided plenty of cover. A fine day.

 

CC

 

13

The Battery did a Drill Order under the IG Capt. Asplin. The advance was practised. Great difficulty was found in siting OPs as the land is so flat. A church tower being the only possible view point. A fine day.

 

CC

 

14

Gunners did anti-tank gun drill in the morning and Nos 1 did director work. Officers did observation of fire from the Church tower at Great Ellingham under the IG. A fine day.

 

CC

 

15

Individual training in the morning and afternoon. All officers did observation of fire under the IG in the evening. A fine day.

 

CC

Individual training in morning and afternoon. A fine day.

Maintenance and inspections in morning. Half Holiday in afternoon. A fine day.

 

CC

Church Parade in the morning. BC and Tp Commanders reconnoitred the lying up area at Pinkney Hall. A fine day.

19

Moved at 1400 hrs independently to the lying up area at Pinkney. Arrived there at 1700 hrs. The men slept in farm buildings and the officers in a loft belonging to Pinkney Hall. A little rain.

Narborough

20

The Battery did Anti-tank shooting in a gravel pit at South Rangenham with a Bren gun attached to a 25 Pdr. This proved to be very useful practice. The 151 Fd Regt moved out this morning and the 384 Bty moved in in the afternoon. All Operational orders were handed over. A fine day.

 

CC

 

21

A forward Body exercise was carried out in the West Massingham Common area. Movement was a bit slow at first but C Tp moved up into the second position very quickly. Wireless gave a little trouble in the second position. A fine day.

 

CC

 

22

Individual training was carried out in the morning and afternoon. Officers did miniature range. Slight rain.

 

CC

 

23

Individual training in morning and afternoon. Wet.

 

CC

Maintenance in the morning. Half Holiday in the afternoon. Slight rain.

 

CC

 

25

Church Parade was in the camp a short service was held in 384 Bty wagon lines. Rain in afternoon.

 

CC

 

26

Individual training. The Regiment was visited by the GOC 46 Division. A fine day.

 

CC

 

27

Moved to Didlington Camp independently to take over from the 71st Fd Regt. The start point was passed at 1330 hrs and the journey was made in 1½ hours.  The whole of the 220 Bde is encamped in and around Didlington Park. It is a hutted camp in the woods with very few roads. Showers.

 

CC

 

28

We started “helson” exercise today. The beginning was rather like the end – very slow and tedious. In the afternoon, after receiving orders from the Colonel, The R&O groups moved over to a position about 2 miles North of the camp, and in a leisurely way prepared for the morrows battle. Since our role was highly defensive, regimental survey was of importance, and we were put on a Regtl Grid and a number of CP tanks were nominated. Being, for the purpose of the exercise, Germans, these had to be fired with GAS – the first time our chemical range tanks have been called for. Whether unobserved Chemical shoots without meteor have any hope of being effective seems doubtful, but anyway, the Command Post went through the necessary routines. Since only Bty staff and signallers went out on the exercise it was possible to get everyone under cover for the night, and the cooks were able to liaise with those of another Regt and use their cookhouse so their department went well too.

 

FGMM

 

29

Up at dawn. After yesterday’s lovely weather – almost the only hot day in May – it was disappointing to see a grey, drizzling sky which held no promise of anything but more rain to come. Contact was gained with the enemy at about 0730 hrs and on instruction from the Ops DHQ, we fired some of the gas concentrations worked out the previous evening. The 23 RFs whom we were supporting, had an ??? line ahead of the FDLs and we had an FOO with it. This gradually caved in and by midday contact had been gained on the FDL. At about this time an intelligence report was received by RHQ to the effect that an enemy HQ was established in the YMCA canteen in the market place in Swaffham, so 2 guns from D troop were sent to a forward position and 50 rds of HE and gas were fired at it. Orders for the move back came about 1215, and the CPO went back to find a new Bty point in East of Thetford. The regiment moved out at 1630 hrs, the Bty recorded 2Ls in the new position at about 1745 hrs. The new position – around Field Barn – suited the weather which by this time was thoroughly unpleasant – for there was an excellent ??? building in which everyone was accommodated. Sentries were posted with care owing to the possibility of enemy patrol activity, and a good night was spent by all.

 

FGMM

 

30

Up at dawn again, to see yet another unpromising day as far as the weather was concerned. The line to “D” OP was working very badly, and was narrowly out by our own carriers. I don’t see how this can be helped, as it is impractical to have the line entirely over head or bury it since there is not the time, so I suppose the linesmen have just got to keep busy. Normally, wireless would have been the solution, but we were a set short owing to them being repaired. The colonel of the 23 RF cooperated very well with the OPs, but they reported that Junior Commanders had little appreciation of the function of the Field Artillery. A lot of messages came through the Command Post, and the machines seemed to be working well. There seems to be a tendency on the part of those who arrange fire plans with the infantry off a 1” map to forget the scale, and to give area shoots to the CP, which bear no relation to the effective coverage of a Bty. The battle was fast and furious during the morning with a very fluid position where at times our armoured OP was behind the enemy. Under the guidance of Capt. Robertson, however, it avoided capture and remained in action until the end of the battle. Lieut R. Jones, however, who was commanding D Troop was not so fortunate, for his OP was overrun, and he died, according to his own estimate, fighting gallantly with a revolver in either hand. The 23 RF was very successful in repelling the attacks of a much superior force, and at 1400 hrs when the ceasefire sounded, were reasonably intact, although the enemy were within 1500 yards of the Bty position. The rest of the day was not so interesting, for it was not until 1900 hrs that we were allowed to return the 14 miles that separated us from camp and our dinners.

 

FGMM

 

31

The morning was spent in maintenance and inspections. Laying practice for gunners. Half holiday in the afternoon. A fine day.
