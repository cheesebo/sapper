War Diary

                                                                                                                             

384th Field Battery RA 142nd Field Regiment RA

 

PLACE

DATE

 

 

Basildon

1 Mar 1941

Morning was spent in maintenance and inspections, and preparing the wagon lines which have become very muddy owing to heavy rain. A message was received in the evening that the remaining 5 25pdrs were ready for collection at Donnington, Shropshire. Six tractors and spare drivers under Lt Roland Jones were detailed to collect them on Sunday. A wet day.

 

CC

 

2

The morning was spent in digging slit trenches & the party detailed to collect the guns left at 1100 hrs. Showers.

 

CC

 

3

A new experiment in PT for the regiment was tried taking place at 0845 hrs after breakfast. This will take place at the same time every second day. Fatigues again took up the greater part of the day, and road making for the battery waggon lines was again the main job. The CRA and Divisional General both made a tour of the regimental area, and the billets were especially spruced up for the visit. Major Chichester and self reconnoitred a “hide” for the Regiment in the Wokingham area. Major Chichester left after duty for his week’s leave. Capt AD Stoddart took command of the Battery. The 5 18/25 pounders arrived safely after dark.

 

ADS

 

4

The morning was spent in taking over & cleaning the new guns and although several minor stores are still missing, Nos 1 and their detachments were all delighted to get a gun of their own. Being Battery on duty not many men were available, and the few extra were employed on road making in the waggon lines, which are improving, though still very muddy.

 

ADS

 

5

After PT after breakfast, both troops went off independently to the Cold Ash Common area & practised gun drill coming into action & anti-tank drill. The signallers under 2/Lt Barnaby, and the Acks under 2/Lt Stead also went off for a day’s exercise, the whole battery returning independently about 1630 hrs. Haversack rations were taken. A useful day from the training point of view and one to be repeated again as soon as possible. A lovely almost Spring day. New intake (about 53) arrived for the regiment.

 

ADS

 

6

Preparations for the Bde exercise were carried out throughout the morning, and stores loaded & vehicles got ready. After dinner the Regiment moved to their assembly area at Easthampstead Park, which was reached without incident at about 1730 hrs. The men then had their tea, and Lt Robertson & self reconnoitred the route to the Bde SP & found it would take ½ hour for the regiment to get there. The O GPs were ordered to form up by 2030 hrs: By this time it was raining heavily, and very dark. The O GPs passed the SP in good order & we had a filthy drive under very bad conditions. Orders were given out by the Bde Cmdr at 2400 hrs on the road & 384th Bty were put under command 9th Devons & given a lying up area.

 

ADS

On the road

7

There was considerable confusion at the DP and the guide I was following to the lying up area went off so fast that I lost him. I eventually found the lying up area but as nobody was there I chose a place for the Battery myself. Lt Lloyd had been left behind at the DP to guide the Bty in case the TP BSMs could not get back in time. Actually both TP BSMs bicycles broke down at the DP, but L/Bdr Worth, my orderly, showing great initiative, managed to guide the Battery to the right place, and at about 0145 hrs, I was able to report to RHQ all present & correct except for 1 gun (Sgt Darch ditched) and Y1 (ditched). The 9th Devons were in great difficulties getting their Btn in. Cooking for the day’s breakfasts was immediately started & it was eaten about 0530 hrs. At 0700 hrs the Btn Cmdr held a conference & gave orders for the attack & the Battery were immediately ordered to deploy in close vicinity of lying up area.

Zero hour was fixed for 0930 hrs at which time the attack went in with Arty support from the Battery. Unfortunately wireless between the armoured OP & the Bty was bad & fire orders from this source did not get through. At 1130 hrs the battle ceased, and the battery were ready to move off at 1215. Orders for the return were received & the Regiment moved off about 1345 hrs. It was still raining having never stopped for over 24 hrs, but in spite of being tired, cold & wet the men remained cheerful. Billets were reached by 1830 hrs. It was a very useful exercise under terrible conditions & the morale of the Battery was very high.

 

ADS

Basildon

8

A late breakfast at 0830 hrs after the exercise & first parade at 0930 hrs after which the morning was spent in maintenance. The Bde Cmdr held a conference at Bde HQ on the exercise & Tp cmdrs attended. Half holiday.

 

ADS

 

9

Regimental Church Parade at 1015 hrs for the CO. Marched to Lower Basildon Church. Rather an uninspiring service which was a pity. The CO complemented the parade on their turn out after the mud they had been through on the exercise.

 

ADS

 

10

After PT as we were the Battery on duty, and consequently had very few men on parade. Maintenance & cleaning up the waggon lines, and transferring the MT office, Signal & Survey stores to a hut occupied most of the morning. The waggon lines are in an appalling state after all the rain. Anti-tank gun drill under BSM Jennings for the men left on duty. The new organisation of the Battery occupied most of my day – i.e. trying to sort people in their various jobs. Fine day for a change. 2/Lt Chappell went on 7 day’s leave.

 

ADS

 

11

Both troops went off on a day’s outing with their respective troop commanders, and did coming into action and anti-tank drill schemes. The signallers and surveyors had an exercise on their own which was quite useful. Lt Robertson C troop commander was promoted to Captain. 2 new 30 cwts and 7 new 15 cwts were taken over by the Battery. Equipment seems to be rolling in at last. A fine day.

 

ADS

 

12

Regimental exercise to Easthampstead Park. The battery moved off as serial 3 in the column, and the lying up area was reached about 1220 hrs. Haversack lunches were then eaten and at 1300 hrs anti-tank gun drill took place. The move back began at 1400 hrs and billets were reached about 1615 hrs. The object of the exercise was to learn the way to this assembly area “backwards” and to practise MT movement. All the new vehicles recently issued to the battery were taken to run them in. A fine day though very cold East wind.

 

ADS

 

13

Another fine day this time warmer with a real touch of spring in the air. As we were on duty very few men were available for duty. The new draft of 19 men and 1 Tp BSM were posted to this Battery from a defence Regt. The Troop SM being posted to Charlie Troop and Sgt Braund who had been acting so well as Tp SM returned as No 1 to Charlie Troop. Enough ammunition to make us up to 1st line strength arrived. Major Chichester arrived back from 7 days leave.

 

ADS

 

14

Troop training in the morning and afternoon. A fine day.

 

CC

 

15

Stores were loaded for the Divisional Exercise “Hengist”. Half holiday in the afternoon. A fine day.

 

CC

 

16

Loading stores and maintenance for the exercise. A fine day.

 

CC

 

17

Today at 0805 the Battery moved out on its first big exercise of the war. 134 Inf Bde plus the 15 Recce Bn were allotted the role of German Troops who had successfully landed, penetrated well into East Anglia, and whose duty was to delay British retaliatory action until Panzer Divisions had been landed. Everyone got up in the neighbourhood of 5am, and the Battery had no difficulty in falling into its allotted place in the Regimental column. The move to the Bde SP in the neighbourhood of Marlow was tedious, but once past this the column started to move well. Owing to, presumably unmalicious, fifth column activity in Luton, where a  Provost sign was altered at a road junction, the gun group got on to the wrong road, but the mistake was noticed by 2/Lt Grist who led it back onto its right route with little delay. The only casualty was H Truck, which, after the habit of Fords, broke a back axle. It was towed to its destination and handed over to the LAD. At about 6:30pm the column arrived at Little Brock House Farm, near Saffron Walden, which had previously been reconnoitred by Capt Chamberlain, and settled itself in for the night. Accommodation consisted of rooms for about 70 men in the farm house, and a reasonably commodious farm. A hot meal was prepared, guards posted and everyone, except Capt Stoddart, who was acting BC settled in for the night. Capt Stoddart attended a conference at 2:30am.

 

 

 

18

The day started with orders at 5am, at which the tactical situation was disclosed, and the route for the approach march detailed. In this regard, only 6 sets of maps were available which made orders and subsequent operations unnecessarily complicated. 384 Btys role today was to support the 15 Recce Bn and we arrived, as detailed, at the SP at 6.23 to find that the Recce had been reduced to 4 Staff cars only, the remainder having gone astray – owing perhaps, to the same handicap as we ourselves experienced. The night had been cold, with a touch of frost, but dawn came up very clear, which was a great encouragement as we all had dreaded wet weather. On the Bty reaching its forward Rv, the CPO went forward and established a Bty position around Fryars Farm, two miles west of Sawbridgeworth. Three defensive river lines had been nominated by the Bde Commander, on which successive delaying actions would be tougher and the Recce Bn well past the intermediary line without opposition from the enemy. Capt Chamberlain was sent back to reconnoitre a Bty position behind the second line – the river Stort, and after only one target had been engaged – subsequently established to be the identification of Military Police as enemy by an umpire – the Bty withdrew to a particularly well concealed position in Hatfield Forest. Here again there was a repetition of the mornings inactivity, although tasks were silently registered, particularly the river line of a large wood South of Sawbridgeworth, where an intercepted message indicated that the enemy were contemplating bridging operations after night had fallen. At about 6:30pm the CPO was sent back to reconnoitre a position for occupation during the night. Shortly before 7pm Capt Stoddart received apparently reliable information that a formation of enemy carriers had made a serious break through and were threatening his left flank, so decided to move to the rear position. This was successfully occupied by Don Troop by 1045pm, though the gun platforms were rather boggier than had been anticipated and some difficulty was experienced with the tractors.

 

 

 

19

Don Troop position – near Pharisee Green proved too far back for the tactical situation, and at dawn C Troop which had reconnoitred and occupied a forward position during the night – rejoined the battery, which then moved to Greens Farm, a mile North of High Easter. This position was easy to occupy though the troops were rather close together, and the Battery was ready for action by 0840hrs. By 0930 hrs the battle was in full swing, and for the first time the Battery Command Post got practical experience of its function as collecting centre of intelligence. The OPs were very active and, apart from a good deal of observed shooting, a number of HF tasks were given. A good example of the “fog of war” was provided by the very circumstantial report by Sgt. N. Bath that he had passed enemy troops in hiding near a road 2 miles NE of Greens Farm. Further reports indicated that the enemy were working round to the rear, and both the CPO & a signaller claimed to have observed a small detachment of enemy less than 1500x from the Bty position. Immediate steps for local defence were taken and one gun per troop was sited to cover the rear in case of attack by enemy AFVs. The position was then explained to the umpires, who categorically denied that the enemy were nearer than Gt Dunmow – 4 miles away – and no explanation of the earlier reports could be obtained. Cease fire came at 17.45 and the Bty was on the road at the Regimental SP by 1900 hrs. There was some confusion about the petrol supply, and vehicles ultimately had to move off with insufficient petrol for the journey back to Basildon, supplies from Bty B echelon being promised for such vehicles as ran dry on the road. The route back bypassed London by the North Circular Road, and it was with some interest that we observed, in approaching London, that a major “Blitz” was in progress. The AA fire was most spectacular, and a lot of bombs were being dropped, but the column suffered no damage. Part of the column went astray for a short distance near Epping and in doing so lost contact with the petrol truck. This necessitated supplies being obtained from an RASC depot near the Gt N road, and delayed the column for about two hours. In the end, we reached Basildon by 0730 hrs, and, with the exception of a burst tyre on an imp??? lorry, no MT  casualties were sustained. All agree that this has been a very instructive exercise which was enjoyed by officers and men alike.

 

 

 

20

The Battery spent the afternoon in maintenance and preparing for the anti-tank shoot which is to take place at Larkhill. A fine day.

 

CC

 

21

The Battery proceeded to West Down in Regimental column, arrived at the destination about 1430hrs after an uneventful run. A fine day.

 

CC

 

22

384 Bty shot first in the morning. The shooting was not particularly good and the gunners got a little flustered by the crowds of spectators which were surging round the detachments. However several direct hits were scored and a good lesson was learnt. A wet dull day with a thick mist in the morning.

 

CC

 

23

Sunday morning was spent in maintenance for the guns and vehicles. Rain.

 

CC

 

24

Both troops were taken out on to the Downs for gun drill and coming in and out of action. A very profitable morning was spent a dull day.

 

CC

 

25

Gun drill in the morning and erecting of camouflage. Signallers did an exercise under Lt Barnaby. Gas drill in the afternoon.

 

CC

 

26

Troop training in the morning. A cross country run was held in the afternoon. Officers attended a conference at Chesham in which the CRA discussed several points on training. A wet day.

 

CC

 

27

The Battery did gun drill on the downs in the morning, a very useful morning was spent coming in and out of action. Aiming practice and rifle drill was continued in the afternoon. A cold day.

 

CC

 

28

All vehicle and gun nets were rescrimmed. Battery commanders went on reconnaissance of lying up areas in Essex and Suffolk. A fine day with slight showers.

 

CC

 

29

The whole Battery available went to shoot on the open range near Aldershot. The practice though not of a very high standard was a useful exercise.

 

CC

 

30

The morning was spent in preparing for the 134 Inf Bde exercise which begins on Monday. A dull day.

 

CC

 

31

The morning was spent in preparation for the 134th Infantry Brigade exercise and stores were loaded & vehicles maintained. Dinner had been arranged for 1200 hrs and it was anticipated the move would begin about 1315 hrs from the billeting area. At 1215 Major Mardon who was acting as Regimental Cmdr and Major Chichester as 2nd I/C returned from the Brigade Commander’s Conference and gave orders for the move. 384th Battery were put under command 6th Devons, and ordered to proceed with them from their billeting area in Henley, as a forward body, to the Bde dispersal point. 384th left Basildon at 1325 and proceeded via Goring-on-Thames, and Caversham to their rendezvous in Henley. Capt Robertson then took on Don Troop to act with the forward body, and Capt Stoddart as BC reported to the HQ 6th Devons for march tables, route cards etc. Henley was left at 1600 hrs and we had an uneventful journey to the DP and on to our billeting area around the village of Overton. The men found fairly good accommodation in various barns & sheds, while the officers established the HQ in The Red Lion Inn – at Overton. Most of the guns & vehicles were parked under cover in a large field. At 1900 hours the BC went to the Bde Cmdr’s conference at Laverstoke Park. Taking place in a very small room with about 40 officers attending, made it most difficult to mark maps etc and it is suggested that to avoid congestion in future BCs should not attend this conference. The BCs then returned to RHQ in the immediate vicinity for further orders from the Regtl Cmdr and finally left to return to their batteries about 2300 hrs. Orders were then given to the officers and breakfast ordered for 0515 hrs the following morning, it being anticipated that Bty Recce parties would be on the move at 0615 hrs, to take up a defensive position astride the River Itchin in support 8th Devons – in the vicinity of Itchin Abbass.
